# References: Learning Theories and Instructional Design

1. [Constructivism (Philosophy of Education)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constructivism_(philosophy_of_education)) - Wikipedia - Explains how learners actively build understanding through experience and reflection rather than passively absorbing instruction, the foundational theory this chapter identifies as having zero prerequisites in the learning graph.

2. [Zone of Proximal Development](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zone_of_proximal_development) - Wikipedia - Details Vygotsky's concept of the range of tasks a learner can accomplish with guidance but not yet alone, the same "sweet spot" this chapter uses to justify scaffolding and mastery-based pacing.

3. [Rhizomatic Learning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhizomatic_learning) - Wikipedia - Describes the non-hierarchical, community-driven model of knowledge acquisition this chapter renames "Rhizombic Learning," including its roots in Deleuze and Guattari's rhizome metaphor.

4. The Adult Learner: The Definitive Classic in Adult Education and Human Resource Development (9th Edition) - Malcolm S. Knowles, Elwood F. Holton III, and Richard A. Swanson - Routledge - The primary text on andragogy, presenting Knowles's five assumptions about adult learners that this chapter applies directly to its own professional readership.

5. The Systematic Design of Instruction (8th Edition) - Walter Dick, Lou Carey, and James O. Carey - Pearson - A widely used instructional design textbook covering systematic course planning, scaffolding, and curriculum sequencing, the practical disciplines this chapter builds from its five learning theories.

6. [Connectivism: A Learning Theory for the Digital Age](http://www.itdl.org/Journal/Jan_05/article01.htm) - International Journal of Instructional Technology and Distance Learning (George Siemens, 2005) - Siemens's original essay arguing that learning consists of forming and navigating network connections, the source theory this chapter credits for framing a learning graph as a literal representation of connectivism.

7. [Learning Theories](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK562189/) - StatPearls, National Center for Biotechnology Information (NIH) - Surveys behaviorism, cognitivism, constructivism, and connectivism side by side, supporting this chapter's comparison table of the theories' core focuses and classroom artifacts.

8. [Instructional Scaffolding to Improve Learning](https://www.niu.edu/citl/resources/guides/instructional-guide/instructional-scaffolding-to-improve-learning.shtml) - Northern Illinois University Center for Innovative Teaching and Learning - Explains temporary, fading instructional support techniques, directly matching this chapter's definition of Scaffolding as support withdrawn as competence grows.

9. [Mastery Learning](https://teaching.resources.osu.edu/glossary/term/mastery-learning) - Ohio State University Teaching and Learning Resource Center - Defines the requirement that learners demonstrate a competence threshold before advancing, reinforcing this chapter's contrast between mastery learning and fixed-schedule, time-based instruction.

10. [What is Instructional Design?](https://education.purdue.edu/news/2024/01/01/what-is-instructional-design/) - Purdue University College of Education - Introduces instructional design as the systematic practice of planning learning materials and experiences, the discipline this chapter positions as the point where all five learning theories meet practice.
